A Review on Google's Dependency-Based Authentication

Google’s device-verification-based authentication has been puzzling to me for a long time. In my opinion, this mechanism has increased people’s dependency in the authentication process. That is why I call it “dependency-based authentication,” where, to log in to a new device, a person must confirm the attempt from a previously used (trusted) device.

Recently, I have noticed that several other platforms have also incorporated similar methods. Since Gmail is the most widely used email service, and because the Android OS is so common, people have become heavily dependent on Google. Therefore, I believe this dependency-based authentication method should be reworked by Google. What I have found fully justifies the label I have given it.

In the picture below, you can see a mobile phone in its setup phase asking to confirm a login attempt from another device named Motorola Edge 50.

To find evidence supporting this dependency problem, I took an Android phone, set it up with my credentials, and used it for two to three days. Then, I factory reset the phone and, during the re-setup process, I was able to reproduce the issue. The mobile phone shown in the picture is the same device Google was asking me to use to confirm the login attempt — the Motorola Edge 50 itself. How could the confirmation be done with this phone while it was still in setup mode?

Furthermore, the email account was linked to a phone number, but other recovery options were disabled by Google. Even if it were enabled, messages wouldn't come. Thus, there was no way to continue the authentication process even though the password was correct. The email was already logged in on my computer, so I had to confirm the attempt from there before I could complete the setup on the phone.

This scenario raised a question in my mind: Had Google’s security R&D team, responsible for this implementation, not considered this test case scenario? In my opinion, the team took certain factors for granted while designing and implementing this mechanism, ultimately increasing the user burden.

This implementation still confuses me, as it always has since I was first introduced to it — mainly because of its dependency. In my work on authentication security, reducing user burden is one of my main focuses. My research on password security incorporates human behavior extensively and employs systems that perform a significant portion of the decision-making regarding legitimate login attempts.

In my work on password security frameworks, I have incorporated not only human behavior but also device information, along with several other factors, to create legitimate user profiles. There, the help of machine learning will be taken, and AI could be integrated in future to make things easier. The intermediate goal is to develop a centralized authentication framework, while the ultimate goal may be a password-less authentication system.
